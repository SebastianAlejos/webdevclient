History of Teas

Normally, when tea is mentioned, the UK comes to mind, because tea is known as the quintessential British drink. However, not a lot of people know that tea was actually discovered in China.

In the Beginning

Legend has it that a Chinese emperor's servants were boiling water for him while he was sitting under a tree, when the Camellia sinensis tree's leaves mistakenly fell into his water. He decided to try this infusion, and he found it refreshing - and that is how tea was born. (Source: tea.co.uk)

The Visit from the Japanese Monks

When the Japanese monks came to China and tried tea, they liked it and brought the seeds back to Japan. “The Japanese fell in love with the drink and instantly welcomed it into their culture, eventually creating the now-famous Japanese tea ceremonies”. (Source: Lipton)

And then came the Europeans

The Portuguese were the first to bring back tea to Europe from Asia. The Dutch however were wise enough to start selling it. They sold it to the aristocracy, therefore tea was only for the rich.

Finally the Brits

Because Charles II married a Portuguese princess who was known to be a tea lover, all the Brits trying to keep up with the princess' trend started to drink tea, and they are still drinking tea today.

Tea Trading and Consumption

When Europe commercialized tea, it became a high demand. India came into the picture buying tea from China and selling it to Britain but let started to harvest their own Assam tea. Sri Lanka then came into play with Ceylon tea. With resettling of the Dutch in South Africa, tea was introduced to the continent. As more and more people moved around, this drink became a worldwide favorite.

Tea was thought of as a medicinal drink until the late sixth century. Different preparations of tea emerged, with teas ranging from white to green to red and to black. Along with the different ranges people began to add fruits, spices and herbs for tea to have it’s unique medicinal properties. Over time, tea was no longer restricted to medicinal use and was also generally consumed as a beverage but nowadays people are going back to the roots of using tea for medicinal purposes.
